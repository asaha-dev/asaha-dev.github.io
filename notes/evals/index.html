evaluation - measuring progress vs decision support

in this post

what good looks like in practice

I've spent a lot of time thinking about what makes evaluation useful versus just credible. This is how I think about setting up evals that actually support decisions rather than just track progress.

Evaluation drives some of the most consequential decisions teams make. Should we ship or delay? Should we keep investing in this direction, or stop altogether? At its core, evaluation exists to inform those decisions.

Yet when you ask a model or systems team how they evaluate their work, the conversation usually begins with metrics. Teams can usually list the benchmarks and performance indicators they track. What is less common is a clear articulation of how those metrics connect to decisions. Which metric would actually cause a launch to be delayed? Which benchmark would justify additional investment? Which result would make us reconsider the direction entirely?

Without that connection, evaluation becomes something teams report rather than something they use.

evaluation is about adjudication

I find it useful to think of evaluation as a form of adjudication. The purpose of a metric is not simply to describe a system. Its purpose is to help resolve uncertainty around a decision.

Viewed this way, most metrics should have an associated question. What decision would change if this number became worse? What decision would become easier if it improved? If we cannot identify the decision that a metric informs, then the metric is likely serving a reporting function rather than a decision-making function.

Reporting is not inherently bad. Teams need visibility into progress, trends, and system behavior. The problem arises when reporting metrics are mistaken for decision metrics. A dashboard can provide a comforting sense of measurement while doing little to help answer whether a system is ready to launch or whether a particular risk is acceptable.

This distinction becomes especially important when the team building the system is also responsible for defining its evaluation framework. Most teams naturally gravitate toward measuring what they can measure reliably. The resulting benchmarks are often technically rigorous and highly reproducible. However, what is easiest to measure is not always what a decision-maker needs to know.

As a result, evaluation can drift toward demonstrating improvement rather than reducing uncertainty. The benchmark shows that the model is better than it was before, but it does not necessarily establish whether the model is good enough for the context in which it will be used — a challenge that is fundamentally organizational rather than technical.

Meaningful evaluation requires the participation of people beyond the development team. Domain experts understand what failure looks like in practice, often in ways that are difficult to capture through aggregate metrics. Customer-facing teams understand how the system is actually used and where users struggle to build trust. Product leaders understand the tradeoffs between risk, capability, and business value.

Each perspective contributes a different piece of the picture. Without them, it is easy to end up with metrics that the development team trusts but that do not provide sufficient confidence for the people responsible for making deployment decisions.

Ultimately, the most important stakeholder in an evaluation process is the person accountable for the decision itself. This is often a leader who understands how the system will be used, has the authority to block a launch, and will bear the consequences if the system fails. Their responsibility is not necessarily to define the metrics, but to define the question that the metrics must answer.

Once that question is clear, evaluation becomes much easier to reason about. Metrics are no longer collected because they are available. They are collected because they reduce uncertainty around a specific decision.

This is also where launch gates and kill criteria become valuable. When thresholds are defined before results are known, metrics become constraints rather than retrospective justifications. A metric matters because crossing a threshold changes a decision. If no decision changes, then the threshold was never meaningful in the first place.

I have seen variations of this challenge across domains, from healthtech domains to content systems to enterprise software. In many cases, the model performs well on the benchmark that was selected. Yet users remain reluctant to trust the system. Looking back, the issue is often not that the benchmark was incorrect. Rather, the benchmark was answering a different question from the one users were asking.

what good looks like in practice

When evaluation is designed around decisions, the relationship between metrics and outcomes becomes much clearer.

Every metric can be traced back to a specific uncertainty that someone is trying to resolve. Tradeoffs are identified explicitly rather than emerging late in the process. Teams understand not only what is being measured, but why it is being measured and what action the result enables.

Evaluation becomes a shared responsibility rather than a technical artifact owned exclusively by the development team. The people building the system, the people using the system, and the people accountable for the decision all contribute to defining what success means.
