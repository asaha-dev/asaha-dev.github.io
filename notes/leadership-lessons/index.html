Leadership styles are often described as fixed traits. My experience has been almost the opposite. The instincts that made me effective at one stage of my career have often required recalibration as the organization changed, the work changed, and the people around me changed.

For a long time, my default style was hands-on coaching. I stayed close to the work, shaped direction through direct input, and often built alongside the team. That worked well when the organization was smaller and the expertise gap between me and the people I led was manageable.

The challenge emerged when I began leading larger groups of senior specialists. Staying deeply involved no longer amplified the team. The people closest to the work often had more context than I did and my instinct to contribute answers was sometimes crowding out better thinking.

I had to reconsider what I was actually bringing to the room.

What I've come to realize is that one of the most powerful tools available to me for this kind of situation isn't an answer. It's a question. This post is about that shift, why it happened, what I've learned from it, and where it breaks down.

I didn't arrive at this on my own. One of my earliest managers at Google led this way, and watching it in practice—before I had language for it—shaped how I thought about what good leadership could look like.

socratic leadership

Over time I found myself relying less on answers and more on questions.

I hesitate to call this coaching because my objective is often different. Coaching usually assumes a capability gap that needs to be closed. What I am trying to do is often less about teaching and more about helping the team identify the right problem, expose hidden assumptions, or clarify a decision.

The question is frequently the intervention.

This style borrows some inspiration from servant leadership in the sense that my job is to make the people around me more effective. But it is more active than simply removing obstacles. A well-placed question can disrupt a bad assumption as effectively as a directive, while preserving ownership with the person doing the work.

how to practice this style

In practice, questions come before opinions.

Rather than starting with a recommendation, I try to understand how someone is framing the problem. What assumptions are they making? What evidence are they relying on? What alternatives have they ruled out? What would cause the current approach to fail?

When I have a strong point of view, I often describe how I think about the problem rather than prescribing the solution. That provides a mental model without turning it into an instruction. The goal is to influence the quality of thinking rather than the specific answer.

Sometimes I use speculative framing when I am unsure myself. Instead of asserting a conclusion, I offer a hypothesis for examination. That creates room for the other person to confirm, refine, or reject it. Done well, it creates a collaborative exploration rather than a debate over positions.

This approach also helps me think more clearly. The process of asking questions often reveals where my own assumptions are weak or where I lack enough context to form a strong opinion.

why?

First, trust and judgment are what compounds, not compliance.

When I tell someone what to do, I've solved one problem. When I ask the right question, the person solves the problem and gets a little better at asking that question themselves next time.

In fast-moving domains like AI, specific answers often have a short shelf life. The more durable investment is in judgment. Over time, organizations benefit less from leaders making every decision and more from developing people who can make good decisions independently.

This approach also builds trust in both directions. The team knows I am not looking to override them if they arrive at a different conclusion. At the same time, I get visibility into how they think, which is often more valuable than the answer itself.

Second, at scale, directive leadership doesn't compose.

Running a large organization, I cannot be the deepest expert on every problem. The person closest to the work often has more context than I do. Asking instead of telling acknowledges that reality.

The goal is not to abdicate responsibility. The goal is to create a system where expertise can surface regardless of where it sits in the organization. Questions help create that space.

This also creates stronger ownership. People tend to commit more deeply to decisions they helped shape than to decisions that were handed to them.

Third, questions are often the fastest way to discover where the real disagreement lies.

At senior levels, the challenge is rarely a lack of information. More often, different people are operating from different assumptions.

A well-placed question exposes those assumptions far more efficiently than presenting a counterargument. Before debating solutions, I want to understand how each person is framing the problem.

I've found that many disagreements that initially appear technical are actually disagreements about goals, constraints, risks, or definitions. Questions are often the fastest path to making those differences visible.

Fourth, questions surface hidden assumptions in a way assertions often cannot.

Most bad decisions I see don't come from faulty reasoning. They come from accepting a premise that nobody examined.

A question makes the premise visible. An assertion often replaces one premise with another.

The distinction matters because visible assumptions can be challenged, tested, and refined. Hidden assumptions quietly shape decisions without ever becoming part of the conversation.

None of this means I can afford to stay uninformed.

This style does not work from ignorance. To ask the right question, I need enough understanding to know what I don't know and enough domain awareness to judge whether an answer is sound.

I don't need to be the subject matter expert. But I do need to understand the problem space well enough to recognize strong reasoning, weak reasoning, and missing reasoning.

That requires staying close enough to the work to develop a point of view. It requires holding context across teams and domains. Most importantly, it requires being deliberate about the difference between a question asked from informed curiosity and a question asked because I haven't done the work to understand the problem.

The Socratic move only works when it comes from engagement rather than distance.

failure modes I have learnt:

The challenge with this style is that asking the right question is difficult.

A good question can reorganize the rest of a conversation. But finding that question often requires working through the problem myself first. Taking a step back and thinking before asking is frequently more important than asking quickly.

Teams that need decisive direction can interpret questions as ambiguity rather than coaching. They see the question form and assume uncertainty when what I intended was exploration.

People who haven't yet developed the context to reason through a problem can experience questions as a quiz they are failing rather than as a scaffold for better thinking.

Speculative framing introduces another risk. What I intend as a hypothesis may be heard as a conclusion. Instead of creating space for exploration, I can accidentally anchor the conversation around the very assumption I was trying to test.

Senior leaders face an additional challenge: the questions carry weight. A question intended as exploration can easily be interpreted as a directive. "Have we considered X?" can quickly become "We should do X" once it propagates through an organization. The higher the leadership level, the more careful I have to be about distinguishing curiosity from instruction.

Like any leadership tool, questions can become performative. It is possible to ask questions without genuinely being open to the answers. Teams recognize that quickly. When questions are merely disguised directives, trust erodes rather than grows.

learnings

What I have learned is that Socratic leadership is not inherently better than directive leadership. It is simply optimized for a different situation.

I reach for it when problem ambiguity is high, the team is experienced, and the goal is to improve judgment rather than enforce consistency. When urgency is high or execution certainty matters more than exploration, I fall back on more directive approaches.

The broader lesson for me has been that leadership styles are tools, not identities. The challenge is less about finding the right style than understanding when a style that once worked has stopped serving the organization.
