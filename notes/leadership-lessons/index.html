Leadership style isn't fixed - it shouldn't be. Looking back, the instinct that served me well in one context has consistently needed recalibration as the organization changed, the work changed, and the people around me changed. For a long time, my default was leader-coach, hands-on — close to the work, shaping direction through direct input, building alongside the team. That worked.

The shift came when I moved into leading larger teams of very senior specialists, each with domain depth I couldn't match. Staying hands-on at that scale wasn't a strength anymore — it was noise. I had to reexamine what I was actually bringing to the room.

What I've come to realization is that the most powerful tool available to me isn't an answer. It's a question. I often lead by asking. And it's worth being deliberate about what that means — both the moves it relies on and the failure modes it creates. I didn't arrive at this on my own - one of my earliest managers at Google led this way, and watching it in practice — before I had language for it — shaped how I thought about what good leadership looked like.

where socratic sits among leadership styles

Leading by asking is closely aligned with socrating leadership style. This maps most closely to coaching in Goleman's framework, but has a meaningful difference. Coaching is about developing capability: the leader helps someone build a skill or close a gap. In Socrates style, the question is in service of the answer. This leadership, as I practice it, is less about developing capability toward a known destination and more about surfacing the right problem to begin with. The question is often the point. It borrows a bit from servant leadership — the belief that my job is to make the people around me better at their jobs. But servant leadership can tip into passivity. The Socratic version is more active: I'm not just removing obstacles, I'm creating productive friction. A well-placed question disrupts a bad assumption as effectively as a directive, but without the compliance problem.

When I watch myself in conversations, I observe that I ask questions. Not because I don't have opinions — I usually do — but because I've found that surfacing the opinion through someone else's thinking process produces better, more durable decisions than handing it over directly. Assertions come later and only if needed. This also allows me to close any gaps in my knowledge.

Some examples:

Instead of saying "Do X," I like to probe on different angles: What problem are you solving? What assumptions are you making? What evidence do you have? What would make this fail? What alternatives did you rule out?

When I have a strong opinion I'd rather not impose, I first describe how I solve it. That gives the other person a pattern without prescribing it. They can adopt it, adapt it, or reject it. And sometimes speculative framing gives the other person room to either confirm or correct. It's the opposite of an assertion; it's a hypothesis offered for testing.

why

Three reasons I keep coming back to.

**First, at scale, directive leadership doesn't compose. ** Running a large organization, I can't be the deepest expert on every problem. The person closest to the work usually has more context than I do. Asking instead of telling respects that reality and produces better answers. This also leads to shared ownership. Because people arrive at conclusions themselves, buy-in is often higher.

**Second, trust and judgment is what compounds, not compliance. ** When I tell someone what to do, I've solved one problem. When I ask the right question, the person solves the problem and gets a little better at asking that question themselves next time. It also builds a trust equation - I see if they can come to a solution and they know I trust them now to arrive at a solution. In a fast-moving field like AI, where specific answers go stale quickly, the durable investment is in people's judgment, not in their adherence to my decisions.

**Third, questions surface hidden assumptions in a way assertions cannot. ** Most bad decisions I see at work don't come from wrong logic — they come from accepting a premise that nobody examined. A well-placed question makes the premise visible. An assertion just trades it for a different premise.

None of this means I can afford to leave knowledge out. Socratic leadership doesn't work from ignorance. To ask the right question, I need enough understanding to know what I don't know — and enough domain awareness to assess whether the answer I'm getting is sound. I don't need to be the SME. But I do need to be able to judge. That requires staying close enough to the work to have a point of view, reading enough to hold context, and being honest with myself about the difference between a question asked from curiosity and one asked to cover a gap I haven't closed. The Socratic move only works when it comes from informed engagement, not from delegation dressed up as coaching.

where this fails

The failure mode I keep relearning: questions are easy to dodge. When I ask multiple probes, the person on the other end picks the easiest threads and quietly skips the hardest one. Socratic leadership works well if one can identify and press on the important question(s) — otherwise the cognitive cost of engaging with the hard one becomes optional, and people opt out. The fix is simple but requires discipline: the sharpest question - the one that, if answered seriously, would reorganize the rest of the conversation — has to be visible. This is hard in practice because I'm often still working through the problem myself to figure out the right question. So taking a step back and thinking before asking is critical.

Asking and leaving the thread open is also only half a move. The question needs to close with an explicit commitment / outcome — so it becomes something the person actually has to sit with, not something they can defer.

Other failure modes I've watched. Teams who need decisive direction can read my questions as ambiguity, not coaching — they interpret the question form as uncertainty rather than as an invitation. People who haven't done the thinking yet treat my questions as a quiz they're failing rather than a scaffold. And speculative framing can accidentally plant the very assumptions it's meant to surface. What I intend as a hypothesis, a listener may hear as my conclusion.

My rule of thumb is that I use this style when I am dealing with high problem ambiguity and my team's capability is high. I fall back on other styles when that equation is different -- like directive when urgency is high and execution certainty is importnat.

Socratic isn't a soft style. Done well, it's actually more demanding than directive leadership — for both sides. It asks the leader to resist the impulse to solve, and asks the person to do their own thinking. When both sides hold up their end, it's the only style I've found that scales without becoming bureaucratic.
